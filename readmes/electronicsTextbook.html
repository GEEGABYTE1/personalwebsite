Week 3A: Inductance and Faraday's Law

Home ← Back to Week 2

Overview

This week extends the study of magnetostatics into time-varying systems. We explore how changing magnetic fields induce voltages, leading to the concepts of self-inductance and mutual inductance. These phenomena form the core of transformer theory, switching power converters, motor drive systems, and electromagnetic sensing circuits.

Topics Covered

  • Faraday’s Law of Induction
  • Induced EMF in coils and loops
  • Self-Inductance: \( V = L \frac{dI}{dt} \)
  • Mutual Inductance and coupling coefficient
  • Energy stored in inductors
  • RL Transients and exponential decay

Project

Built a simple RL circuit on breadboard using a 1H inductor and 220Ω resistor. Measured the voltage across the inductor during current ramp-up using an oscilloscope. Observed the exponential approach to steady state consistent with \( V_L = L \frac{dI}{dt} \), and verified the time constant \( \tau = L/R \).

Next: this foundation sets the stage for understanding how inductors are used in real-world circuits like buck converters, audio crossovers, and energy harvesting systems.

Week 3B: Vacuum Tubes and Amplification

Home ← Back to Week 3A

Overview

This week begins the exploration of amplification, starting with its origin in vacuum tube triodes. We examine how these devices control large currents using small voltages and how this principle laid the foundation for all analog and digital electronics. From audio preamps to radar modulators, vacuum tubes were the first step in active signal manipulation.

Topics Covered

  • Thermionic emission and electron flow in vacuum
  • Structure and operation of diode and triode vacuum tubes
  • Plate characteristics and transfer curves
  • Common cathode amplifier configuration
  • Load lines and operating point (Q-point)
  • Input-output signal relationships and gain
